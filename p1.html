Project #1 Lost & Found

Description

This project explores the ambiguity in human language as well as the gap between human language and computer language. The figure above represents someone's lost object, a personal item still alive as memory.

Design Process

I began my process with a shy sketch on a post-it, not really as a form of visualizing the object, but instead, a way to materialize my thoughts. The description I had was simple...

"My lost object is used to capture memories. It was black and silver. It was an analog piece of technology. It used film and the product of its captures had to be processed manually before one could see what came out of it. It was an object commonly used by families and professionals alike. The bulk of its build was metal to ensure it was durable enough to withstand relatively harsh conditions or shortfalls."

Here's where my vision own vision of the meaning of a camera, mixes with the description... I see those objects as powerful memory instruments. Knowing a bit about my colleague, my goal then was to transform that sketch into something personal and intimate. Then I decided to make the sketch interactive. How? By loading random pictures of landscapes and such between the image of the object. With a click, you can not only find the lost object but see some of the memories attached to that object. Or at least that I was the intention. That way, our traveler friend, Wafi - who gave us the description of the Lost Object - can travel again.

Reflection

There's an obvious gap between human language and code language. That gap will be even more noticeable depending on who's communicating. Still, what surprises me the most is the gap between our thoughts and the physical and/or technical language. Objects might seem clear in our minds but a simple transposition to a paper, or any other physical (or digital medium), makes us realize how little we know about what surrounds us. It's also curious to me our patterns while communicating ideas that have emotional meaning to us. We tend to describe those objects using sort of a passionate description, even when technical voice would lead us to a better result. To illustrate, we may consider the many versions of analog cameras out there. Their lenses might be in the middle of the camera body, exposed; or they can be positioned slightly to the right/left. There's also the possibility of that lens being covered. Buttons might be positioned differently. Their different materials, textures, colors... No matter what's the case, when we try to describe, those details often go unnoticed.
